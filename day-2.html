Morning: Tenryuji Temple, Iwatayama Monkey Park

We had another early start to the day in order to ensure we got on the bus up to the northwestern corner of Kyoto: Arashiyama. Most of the attractions in this district open at 9am, so I made a quick adjustment to the order on the itinerary, and we started at the one temple that opened at 8:30, Tenryu-ji Temple. It features multiple beautiful gardens, each of a different style, including a zen rock garden, a moss garden, a still lake, and a trickling brook. There were multiple paths to explore and of course, beautifully bloomed sakura. There was a long 'indoor' path along the temple and through a wooden covered walkway, across all of which we walked without our shoes. I really enjoyed seeing the cherry blossoms, plus sakura azalea and other flowers in bloom here up close.

Goshuin collected.

After leaving here, we each got a skewer of dango from a street vendor in lieu of breakfast. Dango are sweet balls of glutinous rice flour on a bamboo skewer. Basically, balls coated in a sweet syrup and dusted with something that I thought was peanut? Yummy. From there we crossed back over the bridge and began the trek up the mountainside (again. This trip was very themed around stairs and hills apparently).

Editor's note: I was being serious about the stairs. They went like that for a while.

But what could possibly be worth all those extra steps? Well, monkeys. There is an enclosure you can enter to feed monkeys through a cage (you are the one inside the cage) but we didn't do that. Hungry monkeys can go there. Some just chill in the human area. Others are out on the mountainside doing whatever they do.

That is, they just chill and do their own thing until feeding time. The man who had previously been mostly repeating the directions to keep your distance, etc, came out and yelled OYYYYYY a couple of times. For the next two minutes, the surrounding area's monkeys made their way up or down the mountain to amass at the source of the call. They congregated at the main human platform, where humans had been shepherded by the staff and spread out along the perimeter. They played the can-can song and threw food out so the monkeys would go around and the visitors could be sure to have a good close look at the fellas. We only stayed for the feeding because it was literally in 5 minutes as of when we felt good so hey, it won't really make for nice natural photos but we might as well. And it was fun enough. There was also a baby which was amusing.

It's a good thing we were wrapping that up when we did, because as we began to descend towards civilization, it began to rain. Fortunately, there was a covered bench along the way that we sat at when it picked up a bit, so we could plan out our next moves. Down in the main town area by the bridge etc, we would be able to take a bus up the hill as planned to a temple and then work our way down. And by the looks of the schedule, we would have time to look around for a cafe or something, too.

Well, that was the plan, and is close to what happened. The rain did intervene slightly. We got down off the mountainside and walked down the path along the river while checking out the street foods and restaurants as we passed them. The rain started to come down hard, though, so we took shelter under an overhang in Arashiyama Park. There was still enough time to try to wait it out before having to head back to catch the bus. That worked out. When it let up, we went back and found the right bus stop with a few minutes to spare. That spare time was spent enjoying some nice cold vending machine coffee drinks.

Early Afternoon: Adashino Nenbutsuji, Lunch, Gioji, Jojakkoji, Okochi Sanso Garden, Bamboo Grove

Side note, we saw a clown outside from the bus. So that was weird.

Anyways, we got up the hill. The bus stop was only a little ways off from our destination, but since this is a town built on a very steep incline, the under/overpass roads made it tough to figure out where to go. On the other hand, what a lovely place to wander.

Once we figured out that there were some stairs to go down, and where they were, it was simple to reach the quiet Adashino Nenbutsuji Temple. There are a few features of note here. The first is that there are >1200 stone heads, many covered in moss. Most of their features were pretty washed out, though, or maybe I wasn't looking at them right.

Significantly less spiritually engaging was their collection of Tanuki statues. Tanuki are Japanese raccoon dogs, a real animal but also a mythical creature in Japanese folklore. They are said to be mischievous shapeshifters, and while the photo I attached does a good job of hiding it, they famously have gigantic testicles. It looks like people rub them for good luck, like one might with a Buddha's belly, based on the one that was stationed outside our hotel.

This temple was also unique in another way: photography inside the main halls is almost always prohibited, but here, there was actually a sign encouring it. It was good to have the opportunity to share a firsthand view of a temple's shrines. There were many beautiful rooms like these across our tour of various temples in Japan, usually for visitors to view but not enter. Enjoy!

Goshuin: Adashino Nenbutsuji (right) and Kofukuji (left), which we visited the next day.

All of this makes Adashino Nenbutsuji a must-see in Arashiyama. However, the feature that originally drew me to it was its bamboo grove, which is hidden around the back. When I learned about it, there was no question that the reduced foot traffic would make up for the smaller size compared to the extremely overcrowded Arashiyama Bamboo Grove. This is the reason it was recommended and why it was such an easy choice to include it in my list. As expected, it was short, but you could get a photo without strangers in the background, meaning it was a big win. Beyond that, it was quiet and peaceful and the bamboo is huge and only some light dappled through and it was all quite nice.

Editor's note: The crowded Arashiyama Bamboo Grove will be discussed more later.

Afternoon: Lunch, Gioji, Jojakkoji, Okochi Sanso Garden, Bamboo Grove

Once we were done at the temple, we went to a recommended restaurant for lunch: Cafe Gabu. It was in this same area, and again, the walk around this quiet side of town was wonderful. The main thing I felt was missing from my itinerary was the rural mountain towns, and this was helping a bit to scratch that itch.

The restaurant was essentially a small cafe on the hill and it had a wonderful view to enjoy while eating outside. Jacob had beef gyuudon and I had their specialty curry. Jacob also had a yuzu soda, and I stuck to tea. It was all good but honestly the highlight was the Japanese sweet potato in mine... it was so good... it was like, unbelievable, honestly.

Editor's note: I still think about that Japanese sweet potato.

After our lunch, we walked down Saga Toriimoto Preserved Street, which we were able to find fairly easily now that we had a grasp of the vertical overlap of streets here. This street has some vendors but we were mainly focused on the Meiji-period architecture. There were also a couple of small temples and shrines we chanced upon on the walk, which led us to Gioji Temple. This also had a moss garden, bamboo, and stairs. From there it was just a little longer down the road to Jojakkoji Temple, which if you can believe it, also had stairs.

These two temples are supposed to really shine in the autumn, as they have many maple trees that turn wonderful colors.

After that was the Okochi Sanso Gardens, belonging formerly to a famous early Japanese film actor. It was a lovely vista and the entry fee meant that even though (it turns out, I hadn't realized) it is right next to the end of the famous busy bamboo grove, most people don't go in there. They also had a pretty tea room to enter and multiple types of tea you could try included with entry, with a relaxing bamboo view.

Unfortunately, the only logical way back to our bus stop to return to our base neighborhood was through the dreaded plague of humanity filling the Arashiyama Bamboo Grove. Fortunately we had a nice bamboo experience already so we were able to focus on moving forward through this one.

Evening: Central/Eastern Kyoto

Back at Kyoto Station, we were able to check another marked location off my list. It wasn't easy; I was warned that it was kind of hard to find and I can confirm there was an entire area of the station that was tucked away such that it was almost exclusively locals who knew how to navigate there. We got to the small outpost of Nakamura Tokichi, a specialty matcha brand, and did our approximation of a tea ceremony. The tea was good, but what they do with the matcha goes beyond that.

Should've bought more stuff from them, but we were mostly focused on moving on from this appetizer to the main meal so we didn't stop back in their shop. By the time we left and wanted to find actual dinner, it became a bit of a scramble.
