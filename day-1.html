Early morning: Fushimi Inari Taisha

We awoke at the booty crack of dawn. That was 5 for me, 5:30 for Jacob, and out of the hotel before 6. Found our local train station and hopped down to Inari station (one or two stops). We saw our first big red gate at 6:15, pointing us in the definitive correct direction. Then, up the stairs to the main pavilion. On the far side, we found the start to the path, and we were walking through the first few of the thousands of gates by 6:30 AM. It's incredible what can be done without coffee. And thank goodness, while we certainly weren't all alone, it really wasn't crowded. We made it in time to have some breathing room the whole way up!

The gates are a cool spectacle and all, but that is only a small part of what is in this huge Fushimi Inari area, and it's the only thing you ever see on social media. It's not a straight shot up the mountain. There are stops along the way, sometimes with a shop and splitting paths, usually with a shrine. There are whole areas to explore that are densely populated with smaller red gates but also stonework and statues and shrines, laid out in a way that from my American experience is hard to compare to anything but a graveyard. We saw a random cat, a heron, and heard lots of birds, mostly crows. And oh boy, there are a lot of steps.

When we reached the part where the path splits and becomes a loop to get to the shrine at the top of Mt Inari, you get to see a beautifully wide view of Kyoto from above. I have heard that this is a turning around point for many people. But why live with the FOMO? We pressed on. Oof the last section is easily the hardest. But we did it!

And wow, by the time we got back down to that overlook turning point, it was packed. It was busy the rest of the way down, and the entry plaza had come to life. Markets were open and people were everywhere. We did a quick scan for breakfast food, and while there were some street food vendors and it was not even 8:30 in the morning yet, it was not breakfast food. We returned and got in line for the goshuin, the temple and shrine stamp/calligraphy mark of your pilgrimage to the site. It opened at 8:30 and moved nice and quickly. We got our goshuincho, the book specifically for recording these pilgrimages, and with that, we had concluded our business at Fushimi Inari Taisha.

Note: Japanese books are read from right to left, so the front cover is on the right side.

Morning: Breakfast, Tofukuji

We set off via side walkway as we headed for our next destination, which would require walking through some residential areas. It was a very nice neighborhood, the houses looked amazing there. Almost immediately we were greeted by some tiny Totoro guys on someone's retaining wall! Kawaii!

It was truly amazing how quickly we transitioned out of one of the most famously tourist-filled landmarks in the city, if not the country, into quiet, intimate side roads. I love how ingrained the shrines and temples are into the real, lived-in neighborhoods, and that we could see both sides of the city without any hassle.

This walk brought us to Tofukuji Temple, which I heard about in two separate posts about "hidden gems" of Kyoto. That was absolutely accurate. It is a huge grounds, and it was practically empty! It's really amazing how little off the beaten path you need to get to escape the tourist crowds. This temple is known for its huge, historic gate, a garden, and a tranquil all-wood bridge. Side note, our hotel's nearest train station was the Tofukuji Station.

Alright, it was after walking around here that we realized we still hadn't had breakfast or coffee (I think we each had a small granola bar at a midway stop along Fushimi inari). We found a small place that was in the direction of both our hotel and our next stop. Jacob tried to figure out which direction that was, and I took advantage of this quiet moment to give Caitlin a quick phone call. We went to Bread & Coffee Hirarinto. It was a restaurant, but it had the feel of being invited into someone's home. It was the first place we had to take off our shoes other than our hotel room. We took a table (there were tatami mats, where you sit on the floor, in the next room). The space had lots of stuff around, on the sills and shelves and walls, in a way that felt cozy. Staff were friendly. I think this was also the first place Jacob actually used some Japanese. Our tag team strategy is that I need to engage the person and then pass it over to him and it ends up working pretty well.

After that, we had a brief stop back at the hotel room. I changed my shoes from the hiking sneakers to my slip-ons, which would turn out to have some pros and some for the rest of the day.

End of Morning / Early Afternoon: Sanjusangendo and Lunch

Our next real stop was Sanjusangendo Temple , which boasts 1001 life-sized wooden statues of the goddess Kannon. Despite them all being of the same figure, sort of, they're actually all slightly different and have their own names and features. They're all documented and organized and itemized in a very impressive museum-like way, despite looking like they've always been there. I'm blanking on the word that I'm trying to think of right now. It was good archival work. Jacob made the comparison to the air temples in Avatar: the Last Airbender, with the statues of all the previous incarnations. Also, in the front on either end, there are the wind and thunder god, respectively, there to protect them. Along the front are 20-something additional statues of other Figures and there were descriptions in front of them as well. Photography was not allowed inside the temple. The grounds had a nice tidy little zen garden and smaller periphery shrines.
